Abstract
The use of ionic liquids (ILs) to modulate protein phase behavior is often hindered by the stochastic nature of nucleation and complex specific ion effects. Here, we deconstruct the crystallization pathways of lysozyme in ethylammonium nitrate (EAN) using a multi-modal Small-Angle X-ray Scattering (SAXS) strategy that integrates high-throughput 96-well screening, in situ capillary thermal profiling, and time-resolved kinetic monitoring. To address the critical challenge of batch-to-batch variability in soft matter systems, we deployed the automated framework that enforces rigorous data curation and extracts physical descriptors (pseudo-Rg and crystallinity index) from a standardized experimental campaign. This single-batch consistency proved prerequisite for training reliable Machine Learning (ML) models. Physically, the data reveal a two-step nucleation mechanism driven by ion-induced oligomerization, alongside a distinct thermal hysteresis where heating serves as a kinetic switch to reset metastable aggregates. computationally, our ML analysis demonstrates that linear models fail to capture these transitions (R2), whereas ensemble algorithms trained on the curated dataset successfully reconstruct the non-linear crystallization zone. By quantifying the dominant role of EAN concentration (>65% feature importance), this study establishes a rapid, data-driven workflow to predict protein phase diagrams, transforming the screening process from trial-and-error to predictive design.

1. Introduction
Protein crystallization remains a bottleneck in structural biology, limiting the throughput of crystallography workflows despite extensive empirical screening[1]. The fundamental challenge lies in our incomplete understanding of how solution conditions direct proteins through competing pathways, distinguishing productive nucleation from amorphous aggregation or liquid-liquid phase separation. While Classical Nucleation Theory (CNT) posits that ordered nuclei emerge directly from a monomeric solution by overcoming a free-energy barrier, increasing evidence supports a two-step mechanism [2-4]. In this non-classical view, density fluctuations first lead to metastable, protein-rich liquid clusters, which then serve as precursors for the structural ordering of the crystalline lattice. However, capturing these transient, nanoscale pre-nucleation species in situ remains experimentally elusive, leaving a gap between theoretical models and practical crystallization strategies[5-8].
Ionic liquids (ILs) represent a versatile class of organic salts that remain liquid at room temperature, offering a solvent environment fundamentally distinct from traditional inorganic buffers[9-13]. Unlike common salts, which are dominated by strong electrostatic forces, ILs—especially protic variants like ethylammonium nitrate (EAN)—exhibit a complex interplay of weaker Coulombic interactions, specific hydrogen bonding networks derived from proton transfer, and tunable hydrophobic effects [9, 14, 15]. Crucially, water-miscible ILs allow for continuous property tuning across a much broader concentration regime than is possible with conventional precipitants, enabling precise modulation of protein-solvent interactions without inducing immediate denaturation [16-18]. While EAN has been successfully deployed to enhance supersaturation and promote the formation of varying protein clusters [19-23], the structural mechanisms driving these outcomes remain opaque. Although previous studies indicate that ILs generally maintain protein fold stability even at high concentrations [24-29], the link between specific ion-induced associations and the subsequent nucleation pathway is largely qualitative. Research has predominantly focused on the final yield or diffraction quality [30, 31]. This leaves a significant gap in our quantitative understanding of how the solvent environment directs the dynamic evolution of oligomers during the critical induction phase.
Small-angle X-ray scattering (SAXS) is uniquely positioned to bridge this knowledge gap by providing a global, time-resolved readout of the solution ensemble [6, 29, 32, 33]. Unlike microscopy, which is limited to detecting macroscopic crystals, or spectroscopic methods that probe local folding events, SAXS simultaneously captures the evolution of particle size and the emergence of translational order [34-36]. Specifically, it can distinguish between the smooth, low-angle scattering characteristic of amorphous aggregates and the sharp Bragg reflections indicative of nascent lattice formation. However, applying SAXS to protein crystallization in ionic liquids presents significant analytical challenges. The high electron density of the solvent and the presence of strong interparticle interactions in concentrated protein solutions often distort standard Guinier approximations, rendering traditional data reduction methods insufficient [37]. Consequently, deconvoluting the subtle signals of pre-nucleation oligomers from the dominant background scattering requires robust, physics-based analysis pipelines capable of quantifying population shifts in these non-ideal environments.
Parallel to these challenges, the analytical frontier of structural biology has undergone a seismic shift in the 2024-2025 period, driven by the synergistic integration of SAXS with Machine Learning (ML) and Generative AI to resolve the ambiguity of protein states in solution. Emerging computational frameworks are effectively dismantling the inverse problem inherent in scattering data, offering new avenues to characterize the very pre-nucleation species that defy conventional analysis. For instance, the development of hybrid modeling suites like AlphaSAXS and SAXS-A-FOLD has enabled the reconstruction of physiological conformational ensembles by dynamically restraining AlphaFold predictions with experimental scattering profiles. These tools provide a high-resolution view of the flexible trajectories and oligomeric transitions that funnel proteins toward productive nucleation rather than amorphous aggregation. Concurrently, the characterization of dense, interacting systems—critical for validating the two-step mechanism—has been revolutionized by physics-informed neural networks and algorithms such as FMAPS(q). By explicitly modeling the structure factor (S(q)) to decouple interparticle interference from form factor scattering, these methods reveal the thermodynamic potentials of mean force (W(r)) that govern liquid-liquid phase separation (LLPS) and crystal contact formation. Furthermore, deep learning approaches applied to raw 2D scattering images, exemplified by the CREASE-2D method, now allow for the detection of subtle anisotropic features in self-assembling nanostructures that are often obliterated by traditional azimuthal averaging. When combined with sequence-based aggregation predictors leveraging protein language models (e.g., ProtT5, AggNet) and gradient-boosted decision trees (XGBoost) that pinpoint surface "stickiness", these AI-driven innovations are transforming the study of protein phase behavior from qualitative observation into a predictive science.

In this work, we address these challenges by employing a high-throughput SAXS approach to dissect the crystallization pathway of lysozyme in aqueous EAN. Rather than relying on qualitative visual inspection, we introduce a quantitative analysis framework—the SKILL (SAXS Toolkit)—to systematically map the phase behavior across a comprehensive concentration matrix. Our approach focuses on three critical physical descriptors: (i) the size evolution of pre-nucleation species, quantified via a robust pseudo-Rg metric designed to track conformational trends even in interacting systems where the structure factor (S(q)≠1) [38, 39]. (ii) the transition from disorder to order, monitored by a continuous Crystallinity Index (CI) that detects the onset of Bragg diffraction before crystals become visible to the eye; and (iii) the underlying oligomeric composition, resolved by fitting experimental scattering profiles to linear combinations of monomeric and oligomeric form factors [40]. By correlating these scattering fingerprints with specific ion concentrations, we provide direct experimental evidence for a two-step nucleation mechanism driven by ion-specific interactions, establishing a rigorous roadmap for rationalizing protein crystallization in complex ionic media.

2. Materials and Methods
2.1 Materials
Ethylamine, ethanolamine, nitric acid, and lysozyme (chicken egg‑white lysozyme) were purchased from Sigma and used as received. Ultrapure water was obtained from a Milli‑Q system. 
2.2 Ionic liquid preparation and solution conditions
Ethylammonium nitrate (EAN) was synthesized by the slow, equimolar neutralization of ethylamine with nitric acid, keeping the temperature below 10 °C to prevent thermal degradation. The resulting salt was dehydrated under rotary evaporation and dried in vacuo at 60 °C for 48 h. Water content was confirmed to be <0.5 wt% via Karl Fischer titration, and purity was verified by ¹H NMR.
To map the phase behavior, a concentration matrix was prepared by dissolving lyophilized lysozyme directly into EAN-water mixtures. The solvent composition ranged from dilute aqueous buffer (0 mol% EAN) to neat ionic liquid (~93 mol% EAN), with protein concentrations titrated between 2.5 and 100 mg/mL. pH was adjusted to 8.0 using a concentrated Tris buffer stock where applicable.
2.3 SAXS measurements
SAXS experiments were conducted on the SAXS/WAXS beamline at the Australian Synchrotron (Clayton, Australia). A Pilatus 2M detector was positioned at a sample-to-detector distance of 2.4 m (yielding q ≈ 0.01-0.5 Å⁻¹) at a beam wavelength of 1.033 Å (12.0 keV). 
Two sample environments were employed to address different experimental need. High-Throughput Screening: The concentration matrix was loaded into sealed 96-well plates with Kapton windows. Data were collected at room temperature in transmission mode (exposure: 1 s × 4 frames) to minimize radiation damage. Time-Resolved & Thermal Studies: For kinetic and temperature-dependent experiments ( °C), samples were injected into 1.5 mm quartz capillaries. This setup eliminated background scattering from plate films and allowed for precise thermal control during in-situ heating ramps. All background subtraction was performed using matched solvent blanks (EAN-water without protein).
2.4 Automated Analysis via the SKILL Framework
To standardize the feature extraction across the high-throughput dataset (n > 300) and mitigate human bias in manual fitting, we developed the SAXS Kinetic Interpretation and Learning Library (SKILL). This Python-based pipeline automates the transformation of raw 1D scattering profiles into a structured matrix of physical descriptors through three parallel analytical modules..
Guinier Analysis). The evolution of particle compactness was quantified using the Radius of Gyration (Rg). For dilute systems, the low-angle scattering intensity follows the Guinier approximation:
I(q)≈I(0)  exp⁡(-(R_g^2 q^2)/3)
By plotting (ln[I(q)) vs q2, the Rg is extracted directly from the slope m of the linear regression, where R_g=√(-3m). Given the high protein concentrations and ionic strength in this study, interparticle interference (S(q)≠1) is non-negligible; thus, we report this metric as an "apparent" or pseudo-Rg, which serves as a reliable proxy for the global size evolution of the scattering ensemble.
Crystallinity Analysis). To discern the onset of nucleation from amorphous aggregation, a Crystallinity Index (CI) was calculated. The amorphous background contribution, I_amorphous (q), was estimated via a rolling minimum filter applied to the scattering signal. Simultaneously, the lattice periodicity (d-spacing) was derived from the position of the primary Bragg peak (q*) according to d=2π/q^*, characterizing the packing density of the emerging lysozyme crystals.
Oligomeric Decomposition (Compositional Modeling). To resolve the molecular population underlying the scattering curves, the experimental intensity I_exp (q) was modeled as a linear combination of theoretical form factors derived from crystallographic models (PDB: 3VM9/7JMU). The SKILL pipeline implements a wrapper for OLIGOMER (ATSAS) to solve the non-negative least squares (NNLS) minimization problem:
I_exp (q)=∑_(k=1)^N▒v_k  I_k (q)
where vk represents the volume fraction of the k-th oligomeric species (monomer, tetramer, hexamer, or octamer) and I_k (q) is its corresponding theoretical scattering profile. This decomposition provides a quantitative fingerprint of the pre-nucleation species distribution prior to the emergence of Bragg peaks.

The pipeline integrates three analytical modules: (1) Physical Gating & Extraction: A pseudo-Rg was calculated via weighted linear regression of the Guinier region ( vs ) within a fixed -window ( Å⁻¹). This metric serves as a robust proxy for global size evolution in non-ideal, interacting systems. (2) Crystallinity Index (CI): Bragg peaks were identified using a peak-finding algorithm on the background-corrected signal (rolling minimum filter). The CI was defined as the ratio of the integrated peak area to the total scattering intensity, allowing for the binary classification of "ordered" vs. amorphous states. (3) Oligomeric Decomposition: Experimental curves were deconvoluted into linear combinations of theoretical form factors (monomer to octamer) using a wrapped instance of ATSAS OLIGOMER. Form factors were generated from PDB entries 3VM9 and 7JMU using CRYSOL.
2.5 Machine Learning Model Development
The crystallization landscape was modeled as a mapping function f:X→Y, correlating experimental inputs X=[C_EAN,C_Protein] epresenting the concentrations of ionic liquid and protein, respectively. Two distinct predictive tasks were established
Structural Regression: Predicting the continuous evolution of particle compactness (Y_(R_g )), quantified by the pseudo-Rg.
Phase Classification: Predicting the probability of nucleation, where a binary target was assigned based on a threshold of Crystallinity Index (CI>0.1) to distinguish ordered lattices from amorphous aggregates.

We employed the Scikit-Learn and XGBoost libraries to evaluate linear baselines (e.g., Ridge) against ensemble algorithms (Random Forest, Gradient Boosting). Unlike linear models, ensembles approximate the non-linear phase boundaries by aggregating decision trees via y ̂=∑f_k (x). The curated dataset (n=96) was partitioned into training (80%) and test (20%) sets using stratified sampling, with hyperparameters tuned via 5-fold cross-validation. Performance was assessed using R^2  and RMSE for regression (Y_Rg), and AUC-ROC for classification (Y_CI). Finally, model interpretability was achieved using SHAP, which decomposes the prediction into marginal feature contributions: f(x)=ϕ_base+ϕ_EAN+ϕ_Protein.


 
3. Results and Discussion
3.1 Concentration‑dependent phase behaviour in EAN-water
3.1.1. phase behaviour 
Across the explored EAN-water compositions and lysozyme concentrations, SAXS patterns reveal distinct regimes corresponding to soluble protein, ion‑induced aggregation, and crystalline ordering. In practice, increasing EAN promotes rapid changes at low q. These changes at low q indicate repulsive or attractive association and/or clusters formation of protein particles, which is related to the size evolution of the protein. We quantify these trends of using pseudo‑Rg extracted from a fixed Guinier window (q² = 0.001-0.008 Å⁻²). This calculation yields pseudo‑Rg values close to the true Rg for well‑behaved monomeric lysozyme in buffer [37], while remaining stable enough to track relative changes when interparticle effects, mild aggregation, or background artefacts are present. For example, the pseudo‑Rg in buffer was ~16 Å, whereas lysozyme at pH 2 (a condition that promotes irreversible aggregation) exhibited a larger pseudo‑Rg of 20.6 Å ESI. However, the maximum extent of the Guinier region is often defined to a qRg of 1.0 to 1.3, that on a Guinier plot (lnI(q) versus q2) generates a linear relationship for a pure, monodispersed and ideal particle sample absent from interparticle interactions. The qRg obtained for pseudo-Rg values in aggregated samples were slightly higher (Table S1).
In the compositional matrix, the individual SAXS curves and changes in pseudo-Rg values show a consistent size evolution. Specifically, samples in the low-EAN/low-protein regime (i.e., 2.5 and 5 mg/mL protein in 0-1 mol% EAN, Figure 2a) present the smallest pseudo-Rg values, which is the characteristic of monomeric lysozyme with Rg below 16 Å. However, increasing either EAN content or protein concentration generally drives growth in apparent size. 
As either EAN content or protein concentration increases, a transition toward larger apparent sizes is observed. Specifically, in the range of 25 to 37.5 mg/mL protein and 1 to 10 mol% EAN, the pseudo-Rg increases markedly, reaching values up to 23 Å. This growth reflects the formation of oligomeric clusters and loose aggregates. The corresponding SAXS patterns exhibit an enhanced low-q upturn, which is the signature of attractive interactions and the onset of clustering. These clusters are often reversible and distinct from the crystalline order observed at higher saturation levels [41]. 
Remarkably, at high EAN contents (10-93 mol%), the pseudo-Rg values remain within a relatively narrow window (~14-20 Å) despite the ion concentration being substantially increased. Rather than uncontrolled growth of protein size, this Rg range of ~14-20 Å suggests the formation of compact, salt-stabilized association states [23, 42]. This is because NO3− is weakly hydrated (chaotropes) and is likely to directly bind to proteins without a large increase in Rg thereby decreasing the net charge of the positively charged lysozyme, and this results in salting-in behaviour of the protein [23, 43]. 
At both high protein and salt concentrations, a pronounced downturn in the low-q region reflects dominant repulsive interparticle interactions (red arrows in Figure 2a), consistent with a concentrated yet soluble state rather than attractive aggregation. In these concentrated systems, this is often attributed to crowding effects [44]. Conversely, a similar low-q suppression is also observed at very low protein concentrations (grey arrows in Figure 2a), where it arises from effective excluded-volume and solvation-induced repulsion between well-separated proteins, rather than from crowding effects.

3.1.2. Bragg peaks and phase diagram
At EAN concentrations of 0.3 to 6.7 mol%, most SAXS patterns display sharp Bragg reflections in the intermediate-to-high q range. These Bragg peaks identify the onset of long-range lattice order and provide complementary information to the pseudo-Rg size features. We then merged the two datasets including pseudo-Rg and the presence of Bragg peaks (i.e., soluble or crystalline/ordered) into a phase map. First, red markers denoting crystalline/ordered samples cluster at different mol% of ILs and various protein concentration. Second, the pseudo-Rg heat map was shown in the range of 11 to 23 Å. Comparing the heat map from Rg with the red markers for the Bragg peaks, the two features were aligned. The regions that show Bragg peaks typically exhibit an increase of pseudo-Rg (generally >19 Å). This is consistent with the nucleation theory [45], in which monomers first form higher-order oligomers or dense clusters and then proceed to nucleate ordered domains. 
Plotting composition as molar fraction (Figure 2b) highlights the chemical tuning of ionic strength and specific ion binding, which is useful for mechanistic interpretation [8, 24]. Converting to mass fraction (Figure 2c) produces a more intuitive phase plot for practical use. Note that IL-water states evolve across four ranges: 0-1 mol% (ca. <6 wt%, diluted) similarly as buffer salts; 1-17 mol% (ca. 5-70 wt%, mixtures) forms local clusters; 17-30 mol% (ca. 70-95 wt%, hydrated) involves ILs in the protein’s first solvation shell as free water decreases; and >50 mol% (ca. 90-100 wt%) creates an ordered ionic network that may strip essential water, significantly impacting protein stability and SAXS scattering patterns [8, 30, 46, 47]. Practically, the wt% phase map on the right more directly guides experimentalists (screening and scale-up) while the mol% representation on the left remains valuable for interpreting ion-specific effects on protein-ion interactions. The crystallization zone often appears as a dome-shaped region in salt wt% vs protein concentration [48]. As shown in Figure 2c, an upper region where the IL content of 10-40 wt% re-solubilizes protein or suppresses crystallization of the lysozyme. In other words, low IL levels promote the attractive interactions and local concentration fluctuations necessary for nucleation, whereas slightly high IL content shifts toward solubilization (the solubilization zone seen in the right panel), which is a salting in behaviour.
Mechanistically, therefore, the phase diagram captures two competing features: (i) growth of monomer into oligomeric intermediates (shown as pseudo-Rg growth and low-q intensity) and (ii) ordering into crystals (shown by Bragg peaks). The appearance of peaks is not simply determined by instantaneous size: many conditions show increased pseudo-Rg without crystallinity, indicating off-pathway amorphous aggregation or kinetically trapped clusters. 
3.1.3. Batch variation and data quality
One must acknowledge the inherent stochasticity involved in protein crystallization, particularly given the operational constraints of high-throughput SAXS experiments. We observed that with standardized protocols, the unavoidable latency between sample preparation and X-ray exposure (often extending 1 or more hours due to beamline logistics) means that samples may evolve slightly beyond their initial fresh state before measurement. As shown in Figure 3, the pseudo-Rg of protein at the same concentration can vary between two different beamtime sessions. These discrepancies likely stem from a combination of this sample aging and the extreme sensitivity of the lysozyme-EAN-water ternary system to environmental fluctuations; minor deviations in ambient temperature, the precise rate of protein dissolution, and the thermal history during the waiting period can all alter the nucleation trajectory.
Furthermore, the viscosity of IL-rich phases can lead to localized concentration gradients that are difficult to eliminate entirely, resulting in micro-batches with slightly different aggregation states. Rather than treating these variations as mere experimental error, we view them as an indication of the system's complex phase space. This variability underscores the necessity of the expanded dataset presented here; by aggregating data across multiple preparation cycles and testing conditions, we aim to capture a statistically representative ensemble of the protein’s structural evolution, rather than depending on individual successful results.

3.2 Temperature‑dependent phase behaviour in EAN-water
The temperature response of lysozyme in EAN/water mixtures reveals a distinct contrast between immediate structural transitions and long-term phase stability. In the in-situ capillary experiments (Figures 4a and b), the real-time evolution of pseudo-Rg during a heating-cooling cycle provides a direct probe of the temperature-dependent solubility and protein-protein interactions. Typically, we observe a reduction in pseudo-Rg upon heating, suggesting the dissociation of metastable clusters as the solvent quality improves at increased temperatures. The ability to track these changes in situ is invaluable for identifying the temperature at which the system reverts to a purely monomeric or aggregated state.
In the buffer control, the pseudo-Rg remains remarkably low (8-9 Å) from 25 to 60 ˚C, because the proteins exist as folded, stable monomers where strong inter-protein electrostatic repulsion suppresses the forward scattering intensity (a structure factor effect where S(q) <1). This results in an apparent Rg or pseudo-Rg that is significantly smaller than the protein's actual geometric radius [38]. In the 1 mol% EAN in-situ setup in Figure 4a and b, the pseudo-Rg is initially high at 25 ˚C (27.4 Å) because crystallization and aggregation are most prevalent at this state. As the temperature rises, pseudo-Rg decreases as these metastable clusters dissolve. However, at 85-90 ˚C, Rg increased again to approximately 27 Å, demonstrating irreversible protein denaturation. This transition is also observed in buffer sample with an increase to ~26 Å at 85 ˚C. This confirms that the in-situ capillary setup is an excellent tool for studying the denaturation temperature through real-time structural monitoring.
 
Figure 4. Temperature-dependent SAXS profile of lysozyme. (a, b) In-situ capillary SAXS showing Rg evolution and denaturation in buffer and 1 mol% EAN. (c, d) Plate-based measurements mapping Rg across protein concentrations, revealing how thermal history resets metastable aggregates into stable species.
In contrast, the 96-well plate experiments (Figures 4c and d), where samples were heated and subsequently returned to room temperature before measurement, offer a window into the irreversibility of certain aggregation pathways. The values annotated on these curves often show a thermal hysteresis; for instance, samples heated to higher temperatures sometimes exhibit larger values upon cooling than those maintained at room temperature, indicating that thermal activation may have pushed the system over an energy barrier toward more stable, larger-scale aggregates or even micro-crystalline nuclei. At 25 ˚C, the pseudo-Rg increases sharply with protein concentration, from 21.7 Å at 2.5 mg/ml to 34.7 Å at 100 mg/mL. This reflects significant clustering at higher protein concentration. Conversely, the 90 to 25 ˚C treated samples consistently show much lower Rg values (11.8 to 19.3 Å) across all concentrations. This demonstrates that while higher concentrations favor aggregation, thermal treatment acts as a kinetic reset. The high temperature provides the activation energy needed to disrupt initial metastable aggregates, allowing the system to settle into a more stable, primarily monomeric distribution upon cooling, effectively overriding the initial concentration-dependent state. 
This dual setup effectively bridges thermodynamic and kinetic insights. In-situ capillary measurements define the thermodynamic boundaries of phase transitions, while the plate-based approach maps kinetic stability across concentration gradients. Mechanistically, temperature acts as more than a solubility switch; it serves as a kinetic tool to disrupt metastable aggregates and tune the final distribution of protein species.
3.3 Time‑dependent SAXS
3.3.1. Evolution from Pre-nucleation Intermediates to Crystalline Phase
The early stages of aggregation are characterized by intermediate states (Figure 5a), where the scattering profiles remain smooth but exhibit a progressive increase in low-q intensity. Immediately upon exposure to EAN, the pseudo-Rg increases from ~16 Å (monomeric buffer control) to ~18 Å within the first 10 minutes. While the in buffer is equivalent to the monomeric radius, it should be noted that the plate-based SAXS setup may yield slightly higher baseline values (~16 Å) compared to flow-cell configurations [25]. This rapid initial increase in EAN is consistent with ion-induced association, likely driven by the binding of nitrate ions to the lysozyme surface [23], which partially screens the electrostatic repulsion and promotes the formation of metastable clusters..

(b) Scattering patterns of crystal nuclei and micro-crystals characterized by the emergence of Bragg peaks. (c) Evolution of the pseudo-Rg over time; the red arrows indicate the transition from the monomeric state (~16 Å) to aggregated intermediates (~20 Å) and finally to the crystalline phase (~28 Å). Measurements were performed across various protein concentrations (5-20 mg/mL) to confirm time-dependent growth.
Over a period of 20 min, these intermediates evolve further, with the pseudo-Rg increase toward ~20 Å (Figure 5b). To put this into perspective, lysozyme incubated at pH 2 typically forms irreversible aggregates with a pseudo- of approximately 20.6 Å (Figure S1) [49]. The fact that our EAN intermediates reach similar dimensions suggests a high degree of pre-nucleation packing. In these intermediate groups, the absence of Bragg reflections indicates that while the local protein concentration is high within these clusters, they lack long-range translational order.
The transition to the crystalline state is marked by a dramatic shift in the scattering logic (Figure 5b). Once the induction period (typically > 30 min) is surpassed, sharp Bragg peaks emerge, superimposed on the diffuse scattering. Notably, the pseudo-Rg of these crystalline samples (Group as Crystals) undergoes a substantial shift from ~20 Å to ~28 Å. This apparent size increase in the Guinier region is not only a reflection of individual particle growth but indicates the onset of inter-particle interference and the formation of ordered nuclei that scatter collectively. The contrast between the steady, slow growth of non-crystalline intermediates and the step-change observed upon peak emergence highlights the transition from a stochastic aggregation process to a directed crystallization event.
3.3.2. Crystal Signatures and Packing Trends from Bragg Peak Analysis
Automated extraction of peak parameters (as implemented in our Step 2 script) allows us to quantify the lattice properties of the resulting crystals. The primary Bragg peaks observed in EAN consistent with a monoclinic symmetry [36], a common polymorph for lysozyme grown in the presence of specific anions. By analyzing the peak widths using a Scherrer-based approach, we estimate the crystalline domain sizes to be in the range of ~200 nanometers, suggesting that the Crystals group in Figure 5b consists of a population of micro-crystals rather than a single macroscopic lattice.
The d-spacing derived from the primary peak at around 0.15 Å-1 remains relatively stable during the growth phase, indicating that once the initial lattice is established, the unit cell parameters do not significantly contract. However, the Crystallinity Index (CI), i.e., the ratio of peak area to total scattering, continues to increase, confirming that the transformation is driven by the recruitment of remaining monomers and oligomers from the bulk solution into the growing crystalline domains (Figure 5c). 
3.3.3. Two Step Nucleation Pathway and Oligomeric Population Dynamics
To provide a molecular-level interpretation of these transitions, we utilized the OLIGOMER fitting pipeline (Step 3) to decompose the scattering patterns into populations of monomers and symmetric oligomers (derived from PDB 3VM9 and 7JMU). The fitting results (Figure 5d and e) provide strong quantitative evidence for a two-step nucleation mechanism.
SAXS curves were decomposed into monomer and oligomer contributions using the OLIGOMER tool (Fig. 5d). These fits (data in black, fits in red) assume model form factors for monomers, tetramers, hexamers, etc., derived from the known crystal structure. The corresponding volume fractions are plotted in Fig. 5e. Initially (buffer) the solution is 100% monomer. By 22 min the monomer fraction has decreased to ≈88%, with small fractions of tetramer (~5%), hexamer (~2%), octamer (~5%). The apparent Rg noted at the top of Fig. 5e increases in parallel with these oligomer populations. These results show that even near nucleation the bulk of protein remains monomeric; only a few percent of larger oligomers form and drive the Rg increase. The high-quality fits confirm that lysozyme oligomers begin to appear during stage I, but most molecules stay monomeric until stage II when crystals form. This multi-component analysis follows established SAXS practice: one deduces the solution oligomeric state by matching experimental scattering to theoretical curves of candidate oligomers[40]. This transformation of monomers into growing oligomers over time is consistent with previous findings observed by Dynamic Light Scattering (DLS) [39].
As the system enters the Crystal phase, the monomer population drops significantly (from 88% down to 44% in the most crystalline samples while the octamer fraction rises sharply). Note that in the OLIGOMER fitting of crystalline samples, the octamer population likely acts as a proxy for the crystalline lattice itself or highly ordered pre-nucleation clusters that the program cannot distinguish from a discrete octamer. The poor fitting (high χ2 shown in Figure S4) at very low is a sign of large-scale order that exceeds the size limit of the structural library used for fitting.
These findings further support a two-step model (Figure 9c). In the first step, there is a rapid, ion-induced formation of a dense phase consisting of tetramers, hexamers, and octamers. This phase acts as a reservoir, increasing the local chemical potential of the protein. Second, structural reorganization occurs within these dense clusters to form ordered nuclei. Once a critical nucleus size is reached, the system proceeds to rapid growth, characterized by the emergence of Bragg peaks and the decrease of the monomer population.
3.4. Machine Learning Framework for SAXS Analysis and Crystallization Prediction
3.4.1. Data Curation, the SKILL Pipeline and Predictive Modeling
To address the inherent stochasticity of lysozyme nucleation in EAN-water mixtures, we developed a systematic machine learning workflow anchored by the SKILL (SAXS Toolkit) framework. Recognizing that batch-to-batch variations, trace impurities, and interfacial effects at capillary or well walls can lead to divergent induction times even for identical compositions, we implemented a rigorous curation protocol to ensure the integrity of our predictive features.
Initially, a library of over 300 SAXS patterns was collected. To harmonize the dataset across different instrumental configurations, we truncated the -range to avoid background artifacts from plate-sealing films and excluded samples with non-physical parameters (e.g., pseudo-Rg < 12 Å) arising from over-subtraction of high-scattering ionic liquid backgrounds. After filtering for high signal-to-noise ratios and averaging replicates, a refined set of 96 high-quality, representative patterns was selected. Crucially, to mitigate the batch-dependent inconsistencies described in Section 3.1.3, this final training set was curated primarily from a single, coherent experimental campaign identified as the most complete and stable for computational modeling. This curated dataset spans the continuum from monomeric solutions to mature crystalline phases.
As illustrated in Figure 6a, the SKILL framework bridges the gap between high-dimensional reciprocal-space data and machine learning by automating the transition from raw scattering profiles to a structured, physically grounded feature matrix through four phases:
	Dimensionality Reduction (Steps 1 & 2): To prevent over-fitting common with raw curve inputs, SKILL extracts global size descriptors (pseudo-Rg) and a Crystallinity Index (CI) to quantify the emergence of lattice order.
	Structural Fingerprinting (Step 3): Leveraging the ATSAS/OLIGOMER core, the toolkit deconvolutes the scattering patterns into population fractions (monomers through octamers), providing a molecular fingerprint of the pre-nucleation state.
	Lattice Parameterization: For ordered samples, the toolkit identifies primary Bragg reflections to calculate d-spacing and domain size, capturing the packing efficiency of lysozyme lattices across the EAN concentration matrix.
	Feature Synthesis (Step 4): These variables are aggregated into a unified "Target Table," where each row represents a unique experimental state and each column a physical descriptor.
By utilizing this framework (Figure 6b), we ensured that our subsequent classification and regression models (e.g., Random Forest and Gradient Boosting) were trained not on raw noise, but on the evolving physics of the protein system. This approach significantly enhances model interpretability, allowing us to trace successful crystallization predictions back to specific oligomeric precursors or ion-induced lattice signatures.

3.4.2. Evaluation of Model Performance and Reliability
To ensure the reliability of our in silico predictions, we benchmarked a suite of eight algorithms ranging from linear baselines to non-linear ensemble methods. For the regression task, i.e., predicting the Rg, the performance disparity between model architectures was noticeable (Figure 7a). Linear regression failed to capture the system behavior (R2 = 0.19), whereas ensemble methods, particularly Random Forest (RF), achieved robust predictive accuracy (R2 = 0.803, RMSE = 1.17 Ǻ). This performance gap is clear, which confirms that protein compactness does not scale linearly with ionic strength. Instead, the interaction between EAN concentration and protein size involves complex, non-linear thresholds, likely corresponding to the transition from solvation to ion-induced clustering. These trends are captured by the different decision-tree logic of the ensemble models, including Random Forest (RF), Gradient Boosting Machine (GBM), XGBoost and AdaBoost. Detailed linear fits and residual plots for the regression models are provided in Figure S5.

For the binary classification task, we defined a threshold of CI > 0.1 to rigorously separate crystalline samples from the amorphous baseline. This cutoff ensures that only profiles exhibiting definitive Bragg reflections, which are indicative of ordered lattice formation, are categorized as positive, effectively filtering out disordered aggregates and background noise. As shown in Figure 7b and c, the top-performing models (RF and Gradient Boosting) demonstrated exceptional discrimination capability, with Area Under the Curve (AUC) values approaching unity (0.95–1.00). This predictive power is explicitly visualized in the Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curves in Figure 7b, where the ensemble models exhibit a sharp ascent toward the upper-left corner. This profile confirms that high sensitivity is maintained even at stringent specificity thresholds, ensuring that true crystallization events are efficiently identified while minimizing false leads. However, high classification accuracy alone is insufficient for experimental planning; the confidence of the prediction must also be trustworthy. We therefore evaluated model calibration, which is a measure of how closely the predicted probability corresponds to the actual event frequency. The calibration curves (Figure S6) for our optimized models remain close to the ideal diagonal with slopes near 1.0, indicating that a predicted 80% probability of crystallization genuinely corresponds to an 80% success rate at the bench.
Finally, to bridge the gap between statistical metrics and experimental utility, we employed Decision Curve Analysis (DCA). Unlike traditional metrics (e.g., F1-score in Figure 7c) that weigh false positives and false negatives equally, DCA calculates the net benefit of using the model across different probability thresholds. As shown in Supplementary Figure S6, our framework provides a higher net benefit than "treat-all" (screening every condition) or "treat-none" strategies across the relevant threshold range (0.1–0.9). Practically, this implies that using the ML model to prioritize beamtime allocation significantly reduces resource consumption compared to random or grid-based screening.
3.4.3 Mechanistic Insights into Phase Behavior
Beyond predictive power, the machine learning framework serves as a potent tool for deconstructing the phase behavior of the lysozyme-EAN system. By incorporating the model via SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) analysis, we can quantify the contribution of each experimental parameter to the crystallization outcome. As visualized in Figure 7d, EAN concentration is the dominant driver, accounting for approximately 70% of the model’s decision-making weight, while protein concentration plays a secondary, modulatory role (~ 30%). 
This hierarchy is physically consistent with a salting-in mechanism where specific ion binding dictates the energy landscape. By deploying the trained Gradient Boosting model across the full concentration matrix, we generated a synthetic phase diagram (Figure 7e). The model successfully reconstructs the crystallization zone, which is a bounded region where attractive interactions are sufficient for nucleation but not so strong as to induce amorphous precipitation. This diagram is consistent with the experimental one (Figure 4e). Notably, the model predicts that at very high EAN concentrations (>70 wt%), the system exits the crystallization zone and re-enters a soluble or disordered state, a non-monotonic behavior that aligns with the chaotropic nature of the nitrate anion at high ionic strength. In this context, the ML model functions not merely as a black-box predictor, but as a high-throughput interpreter of the solubility diagram. By generating a predictive grid of 10,000 discrete data points (100 × 100 resolution), the model effectively interpolates the experimental space, showing the boundaries of the nucleation zone with a resolution that would be difficult to achieve via manual screening alone. This high-density mapping ensures that the transition between crystalline and disordered states is captured with high statistical confidence, reflecting the robust underlying performance of the XGBoost regressor. 
4. Conclusion
By integrating in situ SAXS characterization with machine learning algorithms, we have established a predictive framework that maps macroscopic synthesis conditions directly to microscopic structural states. Crucially, the disparity in algorithmic performance serves as a diagnostic tool for the underlying physics: the systematic failure of linear models to capture Rg and CI variations (R2 = 0.17) provides compelling evidence that lysozyme crystallization in EAN is not a simple additive function of concentration, but is instead governed by a non-linear interaction network driven by Hofmeister effects.
The superior performance of ensemble methods, particularly Random Forest (AUC > 0.74 for CI; RMSE = 1.17 Å for Rg), not only validates the robustness of this approach for handling tabular scattering data but also quantifies the role of EAN via SHAP analysis. The dominance of EAN concentration in the feature space (>65% importance) suggests that nucleation in this soft matter system is primarily driven by specific ion adsorption at the protein interface rather than simple macromolecular crowding.
Practically, the clinical utility of the model is underscored by Decision Curve Analysis (DCA) and low Brier scores (< 0.25), which demonstrate a tangible reduction in material consumption by effectively filtering out non-productive metastable conditions. Ultimately, this strategy—fusing physical insight with data intelligence—resolves the bottleneck of unpredictability in ionic liquid systems and offers a fresh mechanistic perspective on macromolecular phase behavior in complex fluids.
